The AI Threat Matrix
Why the real AI risks for listed companies hide where nobody is looking — and what this means for valuation.
Prof. Dr. Tobias Blask Academic Perspective & Framework Development February 2026

"When the car was invented, you could predict gas stations. You could not predict Los Angeles." — Andrew Chen (2024)

Gas stations are a first-order effect — obvious, linear, and already priced in before the first Model T rolled off the line. But cars also enabled suburbia, made Walmart possible, shaped the geography of Los Angeles, and rewired the geopolitics of the Middle East. Those were second- and third-order effects. Nobody saw them coming — and they turned out to be far larger than the gas stations.

This is exactly the situation we face with AI today. The sell-side consensus on AI disruption is thorough — but narrow. We model the gas stations well: task automation, headcount reduction, margin expansion. These are the right questions. But they are the easy questions. The harder ones — and arguably the ones with greater impact on equity valuations — are: What happens after direct substitution? What happens when AI doesn't replace the work but changes how customers behave, reconfigures supply chains, and rewrites entire markets?

This briefing introduces the AI Threat Matrix — a framework that classifies AI disruption into three orders and maps them onto eight business model archetypes of listed companies. It separates the gas stations from Los Angeles.

Core Thesis

We analyze the gas stations thoroughly — direct automation, job displacement, efficiency gains. But we need to expand our analytical framework: The Los Angeles effects — shifts in customer behavior, structural market reconfiguration, the obsolescence of entire business models — deserve systematically more attention in equity valuation.

The trigger: On February 3, Anthropic released industry-specific plug-ins for Claude Cowork. On February 5, it launched Claude Opus 4.6 — an AI model capable of coordinating autonomous agent teams and processing 1 million tokens of context. The market response was immediate: Bloomberg reported a trillion-dollar tech wipeout. The S&P 500 Software & Services Index fell 20% year-to-date. FactSet dropped 10%. S&P Global, Moody's, and Nasdaq all saw sharp declines. Fortune headlined: "Anthropic's Claude triggered a trillion-dollar selloff."

The repricing event: On February 23, IBM shares plunged 13.2% — the steepest single-day drop in over 25 years — after Anthropic announced that Claude Code can modernize COBOL, the legacy programming language underpinning IBM's consulting moat. $30 billion in market value evaporated in one session. IBM lost 27% in February alone. This is a textbook first-order repricing: the market concluded overnight that a core revenue pillar — legacy system modernization consulting — is directly substitutable.

The cascade to credit: The repricing did not stop at equity markets. Reuters reported on February 23 that software companies are delaying debt deals as lenders tighten scrutiny. UBS head of credit strategy Matthew Mish: "We expect AI disruption risk to be increasingly reflected over 2026 to early 2027." Tech leveraged loans — $260 billion outstanding, 50% rated B− or lower — are repricing for higher defaults. This is a second-order financial effect: the disruption cascading from equity to credit markets, tightening refinancing conditions for the very companies facing AI disruption.

The cultural shift: Matt Shumer's LinkedIn essay "Something Big Is Happening" (>10,000 reactions) went viral among executives and investors. Boris Cherny, Anthropic's creator of Claude Code, warned that AI agents will transform every computer-based job in America — and it will be "painful." Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei's prediction that AI will eliminate 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs within one to five years featured prominently. Institutional awareness has shifted from curiosity to urgency.

Key Observation

February 2026 is showing the cascade in real-time — in three weeks, from product launch (Opus 4.6) to equity repricing ($1T software wipeout) to single-stock collapse (IBM −13.2%) to credit market contagion (software leverage repricing). The AI Threat Matrix asks: What are the second- and third-order consequences when these cascades accelerate? IBM's COBOL moat lasted decades. It took one blog post to breach it.

Crucially, the three orders are not a menu to choose from. They are a cascade. First order triggers second, second triggers third. Gas station → Suburbia → Los Angeles. And with AI, this cascade accelerates — because software scales faster than asphalt.

The pattern is striking: many sectors show low first-order risk — but high third-order risk. This asymmetry is precisely what deserves more analytical attention.

Content & Media

HIGH · HIGH · HIGH

The full cascade hits hardest here — production, distribution, and monetization are all vulnerable.

Base Case

AI is a production tool, not a replacement. Studios, publishers, and brands control distribution and audience trust. AI lowers costs but human creators retain their editorial authority. Ad-supported models persist.

Central Scenario

Generative AI floods content supply, collapsing ad CPMs. Users shift to AI-curated feeds and summaries — publishers lose reach and the advertising model erodes. Chegg (−99% since ChatGPT launch) is the canary in the coal mine.

Extreme

Content becomes effectively free. AI generates personalized media on demand — articles, video, music — tailored to each user. No one visits publishers anymore. The entire media value chain collapses to the model layer.

Professional Services

HIGH · MED · HIGH

The paradox: the commodity core is highly exposed, but relationship capital provides a partial shield. IBM's 13% single-day drop (Feb 23, 2026) after Anthropic's COBOL tool announcement shows how fast the market reprices when a moat is breached.

Base Case

Regulatory complexity and trust relationships protect traditional models. AI handles commoditized tasks (document review, basic research) but advisory relationships remain intact. Firms integrate AI as an efficiency lever.

Central Scenario

AI replaces research, drafting, and due diligence — the commoditized heart of consulting and legal models. Clients arm themselves with AI and bring work in-house. Hourly rates compress, but relationship-driven advisory persists at the top. IBM's COBOL modernization business is the canary: when AI can break legacy-system lock-in, the consulting moat erodes.

Extreme

AI-native platforms combine automated production with human quality control, collapsing traditional partnership models. The Big Four become The Big Two. Entire junior professional tiers are eliminated.

Infrastructure & Hosting

LOW · HIGH · HIGH

The quintessential cascade case — trivial first-order risk masking existential second- and third-order exposure. → Detailed IONOS analysis below.

Base Case

AI website builders are incremental improvements. Hosting providers integrate them and upsell. Domain lock-in persists, and the SME customer base remains sticky. Business as usual with better tools.

Central Scenario

The zero-click economy erodes website traffic. AI assistants deliver answers without visits — the hairdresser's, plumber's, accountant's websites lose their purpose. SMEs question hosting spend. ~82% of revenue under structural pressure.

Extreme

The hosting paradigm shifts entirely — from website hosting (presentation layer) to data/API hosting (data layer for AI consumption). Hyperscalers dominate the new market. Traditional hosting becomes a legacy business.

E-Commerce & Marketplaces

LOW · MED · HIGH

Reassessed downward in orders 1–2: AI strengthens platforms more than it threatens them — until it doesn't.

Base Case

AI improves product discovery, dynamic pricing, and logistics optimization — all of which strengthen platforms, not threaten them. Network effects and fulfillment moats (Amazon, Zalando) persist and deepen.

Central Scenario

AI shopping agents increasingly mediate purchases but still operate within marketplace APIs. Platforms that control fulfillment retain leverage; pure aggregators without logistics face margin compression. Discovery shifts but transactions stay.

Extreme

AI agents negotiate directly with manufacturers, bypassing marketplaces entirely. The marketplace as "discovery layer" becomes irrelevant. Only platforms with physical infrastructure (warehousing, delivery) retain value.

Financial Intermediation

HIGH · MED · MED

High automation exposure at the task level, but regulation and trust create a structural brake on the cascade.

Base Case

Regulatory barriers and trust requirements shield incumbents. AI improves internal efficiency (underwriting, compliance) but doesn't fundamentally alter the customer relationship or market structure.

Central Scenario

Underwriting, credit assessment, and compliance become highly automated. Embedded finance (checkout lending, API banking) shifts some value from intermediaries to platforms. Regulation slows the cascade — but only delays it.

Extreme

AI-native financial agents handle all consumer finance within platform ecosystems — insurance, investment, lending. Traditional intermediaries become pure balance-sheet utilities, stripped of the advisory layer.

Education & Training

HIGH · HIGH · MED

Reassessed: third order lowered. The credential model is more deeply embedded in institutional structures than initially estimated.

Base Case

Credential value and institutional prestige protect the core. AI tutors are supplements, not substitutes. Accreditation frameworks remain rigid, and employers continue to require formal qualifications.

Central Scenario

AI tutors and auto-grading substitute core instructional services. Students bypass course platforms for direct AI interaction — why pay for a subscription when ChatGPT teaches the same? Enrollment in commodity content courses drops sharply. Pearson and Chegg are the canaries.

Extreme

AI-verified competency assessments begin to challenge traditional certifications. But deeply embedded institutional prestige — universities, professional bodies, regulatory requirements — resists full disruption. The credential fractures, but doesn't shatter.

SaaS & Enterprise Software

MED · MED · HIGH

Reassessed: second order lowered. Enterprise switching costs and procurement inertia are stronger brakes than initially modeled. → Detailed SAP analysis below.

Base Case

Enterprise switching costs and data lock-in protect incumbents. AI features become table stakes but don't threaten the SaaS model itself. Incumbents like SAP, Salesforce, ServiceNow integrate AI and maintain pricing power.

Central Scenario

AI copilots automate workflows within existing software. Some vertical SaaS categories face feature absorption, but enterprise procurement inertia and integration complexity slow displacement. The moat is organizational, not technical.

Extreme

General-purpose AI platforms absorb entire software categories — the "SaaS Collapse." Why buy a CRM when an AI agent handles it natively? Entire mid-market SaaS layers disappear. Only infrastructure-grade platforms survive.

Manufacturing & Industrial

LOW · LOW · MED

Reassessed significantly downward. The moat is atoms, not bits — and atoms resist digital disruption.

Base Case

Physical production is inherently AI-resistant. Robotics automation is incremental and capital-intensive. Legacy installed base creates a long transition runway. AI is a tool for operational efficiency, not a threat vector.

Central Scenario

AI optimizes predictive maintenance, quality control, and supply chain planning — delivering efficiency gains, not disruption. Physical assets cannot be digitally substituted. The industry benefits from AI more than it suffers from it.

Extreme

Fully autonomous factories and AI-orchestrated supply chains restructure global manufacturing networks. Reshoring accelerates. But the timeline is decades, not years — and the installed base creates enormous inertia.

The paradox: IONOS' first-order risk is trivial. But ~82% of its revenue base depends on a single premise — that end users visit websites — which is being eroded by second- and third-order effects. The question for valuation: How fast does this equilibrium shift?

The Zero-Click Economy — Observable Data

This is not a theoretical risk. The second-order cascade is already measurable:

• Global publisher traffic from Google search declined by a third in 2025 (Press Gazette / Chartbeat)

• Google AI Overviews cause a −61% drop in organic click-through rates for informational queries (Search Engine Land)

• Pew Research (2025): Users who encounter AI summaries are significantly less likely to click on links to other websites

The premise that underpins 82% of IONOS' revenue — that people visit websites — is eroding at a pace that is measurable in annual data, not hypothetical over decades.

The Dual Nature: Driver and Target

SAP occupies a unique position: the company is actively driving AI integration (Joule, Business AI, BTP) — while simultaneously being exposed to AI disruption. This duality makes the case analytically compelling.

The Cascade

1. Med AI Copilot & Automation
SAP's Joule automates ERP routine tasks — bookings, report generation, master data maintenance. Manageable for SAP itself. But: the partner ecosystem (~€180B consulting market) takes a harder hit. Low-code/AI tools compress implementations by 30–50%.
2. High Unbundling Pressure
CFOs ask the fundamental question: Do we need a monolithic ERP — or can AI-native point solutions handle individual processes better? Coupa (procurement), Workday (HR), Ramp (expenses) — specialized tools attack SAP's bundling logic. Every module becomes individually contestable.
3. High Paradigm Shift
AI agents orchestrate business processes autonomously — negotiate with suppliers, manage inventory, process invoices. The role of ERP as the enterprise "operating system" is in question.
The Structural Shift

Today: SAP = "System of Record" — the ERP stores transactions and serves as the single source of truth. Value proposition: One system for everything.

Tomorrow: AI = "System of Intelligence" — AI orchestrates processes regardless of where data resides. Value proposition shifts: The best tool for each process.

Valuation question: SAP trades at ~35× forward earnings — cloud growth and S/4HANA migration priced in. Is the structural risk that the ERP paradigm itself erodes beneath this growth story adequately reflected? This is not a forecast — it is an open question that deserves more analytical attention.

Academic Perspective & Framework

The three-order disruption taxonomy underlying the AI Threat Matrix draws on established streams of academic research in strategic management, technology economics, and organizational theory.

First-order effects — direct task substitution — align with the seminal work of Frey & Osborne (2017) on occupational automation susceptibility and the routinization hypothesis of Autor, Levy & Murnane (2003). These effects are well-studied, and the econometric tools for assessing them are mature.

Second-order effects — behavioral changes in customers, suppliers, and partners — connect to the induced innovation literature (Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2019) and platform economics (Parker, Van Alstyne & Choudary, 2016). When AI changes not what a firm does but what its stakeholders do, the disruption operates through demand-side mechanisms that traditional automation analyses miss.

Third-order effects — structural market reconfiguration — build on Christensen's (1997) disruption theory, Schumpeter's (1942) creative destruction, and more recent work on technological paradigm shifts (Perez, 2002). The key insight from this literature: the most consequential disruptions do not optimize existing value chains — they render them irrelevant.

The cascade logic — first order triggers second, second triggers third — reflects the systems-theoretic perspective of Arthur (2009) on technology and increasing returns. AI's unique characteristic, relative to prior general-purpose technologies, is the speed of this cascade: software-based disruption scales at marginal cost near zero, compressing what took decades in previous technology transitions into years.

Methodological Note

The AI Threat Matrix is deliberately not a forecasting model. It is a diagnostic framework — designed to surface analytical blind spots in equity valuation. The scenario approach (Base Case / Central Scenario / Extreme) follows the structured scenario methodology of Schoemaker (1995) and is calibrated against observable market signals rather than probabilistic forecasts. Each central scenario represents our assessment of the most likely evolutionary path, not a point prediction.

Implications for Equity Valuation

We price the gas stations well — direct AI automation is visible, quantifiable, and reflected in consensus estimates. For the Los Angeles effects, we need to expand our analytical framework. Three structural challenges:

1 — Sectoral Silos Research is organized by sector — necessarily. But AI cascades operate across sectors. The hosting analyst tracks web hosting KPIs — the conversational AI developments that change user behavior sit in the tech analyst's domain. We need more cross-sector perspectives.

2 — Temporal Horizon Third-order effects with a 5–10 year time horizon are doubly discounted: in probability and present value. Methodologically sound — but AI timelines compress faster than historical technology cycles. The discount factors deserve a second look.

3 — Narrative Anchoring "AI replaces X jobs" is concrete and narratable — a good anchor for analysis. But cascade effects are more abstract and harder to embed in valuation models. The AI Threat Matrix provides a structured approach to capturing these effects systematically.

4 — Credit & Debt Markets AI disruption risk is migrating from equity to credit markets. Reuters reports software companies facing higher borrowing costs; UBS forecasts leveraged loan defaults rising 3–5% in a disruption scenario. For levered names in exposed sectors, the feedback loop between equity repricing and refinancing risk creates a self-reinforcing downward spiral. $260 billion in tech leveraged loans — 50% rated B− or lower — are repricing. This is the second-order cascade operating in capital markets themselves.

Small & Mid-Caps: Special Attention Required

Lower analyst coverage + less diversified business models + more constrained adaptation resources = the market segment where second- and third-order AI effects deserve the most analytical attention.

Five Screening Questions for Your Coverage Universe

1
Revenue Premise Test: What % of revenue depends on a premise that AI could erode? (→ IONOS: 82% depends on "people visit websites")
2
Consensus Blind Spot: Which order of disruption is the consensus already pricing? (→ Usually only 1st order — the gas station)
3
Switching Cost Profile: What's the lock-in mechanism and how durable is it? (→ SAP: 18–24 month migration cycle. IBM COBOL: broken overnight)
4
Driver vs. Target: Is the company positioned as AI driver, target, or both? (→ SAP: both — Joule drives, unbundling threatens)
5
Falsifiability: What observable signal would change your rating? (→ Define the trigger — don't wait for the cascade)

Bottom Line

The AI Threat Matrix is not a forecasting model. It is a diagnostic tool — an extension of our analytical framework. It supplements the first-order question we already answer well with two more:

  1. Which tasks can AI replace?
    (First order — the gas station. Analysis here is already well advanced.)
  2. How does AI change the behavior of customers, suppliers, and partners?
    (Second order — Suburbia. Here we need more cross-sector perspective.)
  3. Could AI reconfigure the entire market such that the business model is no longer relevant?
    (Third order — Los Angeles. Here we need to rethink.)
