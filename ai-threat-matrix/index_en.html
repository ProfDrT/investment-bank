Issue #1 · AI & Equity Valuation
The AI Threat Matrix
Why the real AI risks for listed companies hide where nobody is looking — and what this means for valuation.
Prof. Dr. Tobias Blask Academic Perspective & Framework Development · MPCM Research February 2026

When the car was invented, you could predict gas stations. You could not predict Los Angeles.

Gas stations are a first-order effect — obvious, linear, and already priced in before the first Model T rolled off the line. But cars also enabled suburbia, made Walmart possible, shaped the geography of Los Angeles, and rewired the geopolitics of the Middle East. Those were second- and third-order effects. Nobody saw them coming — and they turned out to be far larger than the gas stations.

This is exactly the situation we face with AI today. The sell-side consensus on AI disruption is thorough — but narrow. We model the gas stations well: task automation, headcount reduction, margin expansion. These are the right questions. But they are the easy questions. The harder ones — and arguably the ones with greater impact on equity valuations — are: What happens after direct substitution? What happens when AI doesn't replace the work but changes how customers behave, reconfigures supply chains, and rewrites entire markets?

This briefing introduces the AI Threat Matrix — a framework that classifies AI disruption into three orders and maps them onto eight business model archetypes of listed companies. It separates the gas stations from Los Angeles.

Core Thesis

We analyze the gas stations thoroughly — direct automation, job displacement, efficiency gains. But we need to expand our analytical framework: The Los Angeles effects — shifts in customer behavior, structural market reconfiguration, the obsolescence of entire business models — deserve systematically more attention in equity valuation.

Three Orders of AI Disruption

1.
Direct Substitution
AI replaces tasks within the company.
Time horizon: 0–3 years
Visibility: HIGH
2.
Behavioral Change
AI changes what customers, suppliers, and partners do.
Time horizon: 2–7 years
Visibility: MEDIUM
3.
Structural Reconfiguration
Markets reconfigure. Business models become obsolete.
Time horizon: 5–15 years
Visibility: LOW

Crucially, the three orders are not a menu to choose from. They are a cascade. First order triggers second, second triggers third. Gas station → Suburbia → Los Angeles. And with AI, this cascade accelerates — because software scales faster than asphalt.

Content & Media HIGH · HIGH · HIGH

Triple alert. Generative AI automates text production, image generation, and video editing (1st order). Simultaneously, users migrate to AI-generated feeds and summaries — reach and ad revenue erode (2nd order). Long term, the aggregation model collapses: Why visit a news site when the AI has the answer? Chegg (−99% since ChatGPT launch) is the canary in the coal mine.

Professional Services HIGH · MED · HIGH

AI replaces research, drafting, and due diligence — the heart of many consulting and legal models (1st order). Clients arm themselves with AI and bring work in-house; hourly rates come under pressure (2nd order). Third order: the industry consolidates radically — AI-native platforms combine automated production with human quality control and displace traditional partnership models.

Infrastructure & Hosting LOW · HIGH · HIGH

The quintessential cascade case. The direct AI threat is trivial — AI website builders are incremental. But when consumers retrieve information via AI assistants without visiting websites, traffic drops (2nd order). The entire hosting market shifts from website hosting to data/API hosting (3rd order). → Detailed IONOS analysis below.

E-Commerce & Marketplaces MED · HIGH · HIGH

Listing optimization and dynamic pricing are automatable but not existential (1st order). More dangerous: AI shopping agents that buy on behalf of consumers bypass the marketplace interface entirely — Etsy, Zalando, Amazon lose the customer relationship (2nd order). Long term, disintermediation looms: the AI agent negotiates directly with manufacturers (3rd order).

Financial Intermediation HIGH · MED · MED

Underwriting, credit assessment, and compliance are highly automatable (1st order). Behavioral change is more moderate — financial decisions remain trust-sensitive (2nd order). But embedded finance (checkout lending, API banking) shifts value creation away from traditional intermediaries toward platforms (3rd order). Regulation slows the cascade — but only delays it.

Education & Training HIGH · HIGH · HIGH

Second triple alert. AI tutors and auto-grading substitute core services (1st order). Students use AI directly — why buy a course subscription when ChatGPT delivers the same? (2nd order). Third order: the credentialing model itself is in question — if AI delivers knowledge on demand, what is a certificate still worth? Pearson and Chegg are the canaries.

SaaS & Enterprise Software MED · HIGH · HIGH

AI features automate vertical SaaS functions — useful, but no longer a differentiator (1st order). More severe: general-purpose AI replaces specialized software entirely — why buy a CRM tool when an AI agent handles it? (2nd order). Third order: the "SaaS Collapse" — entire software categories get absorbed by AI platforms. → Detailed SAP analysis below.

Manufacturing & Industrial MED · MED · MED

The most resilient archetype. Production optimization via AI delivers efficiency gains, but physical assets cannot be digitally substituted (1st order). AI-driven procurement changes supplier relationships gradually (2nd order). Supply chain reconfigurations are real but slower and less disruptive than in digital business models (3rd order). Medium risk — but not a free pass.

Core moat: Domain lock-in (customers are bound via domain names), one-stop shop for non-technical SMEs, long-term customer relationships (avg. contract duration >3 years).

The Cascade

In a pure first-order analysis, IONOS looks solid. But the cascade reveals a different picture:

1. Low AI Website Builders
Wix, Squarespace, IONOS itself offer AI-powered site creation. Incremental improvement — IONOS can integrate this and sell it as a feature. No substitution risk.
2. High Zero-Click Economy
ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews deliver answers without a website visit. The hairdresser, plumber, accountant — their websites lose their purpose. Why pay €15/month for hosting when nobody visits the site anymore?
3. High Paradigm Shift
The hosting market reconfigures: from website hosting (presentation layer) to data/API hosting (data layer for AI consumption). New competitors (hyperscalers), new pricing logic (compute instead of web space), new product.

Revenue at Risk

~60% — Web Hosting & Domains under pressure
2nd + 3rd order threaten the core business model Cloud segment (~25%) adaptable

The paradox: IONOS' first-order risk is trivial. But ~60% of its revenue base depends on a single premise — that end users visit websites — which is being eroded by second- and third-order effects. The question for valuation: How fast does this equilibrium shift?

IONOS SE — Business model disruption: From the gas station (AI website builders) to Los Angeles (the zero-click economy).

Core moat: Switching costs (avg. migration 18–24 months), data gravity (ERP as central corporate database), cloud backlog >€60B. The growth story: transition from licenses to cloud subscriptions.

The Dual Nature: Driver and Target

SAP occupies a unique position: the company is actively driving AI integration (Joule, Business AI, BTP) — while simultaneously being exposed to AI disruption. This duality makes the case analytically compelling.

The Cascade

1. Med AI Copilot & Automation
SAP's Joule automates ERP routine tasks — bookings, report generation, master data maintenance. Manageable for SAP itself. But: the partner ecosystem (~€180B consulting market) takes a harder hit. Low-code/AI tools compress implementations by 30–50%.
2. High Unbundling Pressure
CFOs ask the fundamental question: Do we need a monolithic ERP — or can AI-native point solutions handle individual processes better? Coupa (procurement), Workday (HR), Ramp (expenses) — specialized tools attack SAP's bundling logic. Every module becomes individually contestable.
3. High Paradigm Shift
AI agents orchestrate business processes autonomously — negotiate with suppliers, manage inventory, process invoices. The role of ERP as the enterprise "operating system" is in question.
The Structural Shift

Today: SAP = "System of Record" — the ERP stores transactions and serves as the single source of truth. Value proposition: One system for everything.

Tomorrow: AI = "System of Intelligence" — AI orchestrates processes regardless of where data resides. Value proposition shifts: The best tool for each process.

Valuation question: SAP trades at ~35× forward earnings — cloud growth and S/4HANA migration priced in. Is the structural risk that the ERP paradigm itself erodes beneath this growth story adequately reflected? This is not a forecast — it is an open question that deserves more analytical attention.

SAP SE — Structural paradigm shift: From System of Record to System of Intelligence.

Implications for Equity Valuation

We price the gas stations well — direct AI automation is visible, quantifiable, and reflected in consensus estimates. For the Los Angeles effects, we need to expand our analytical framework. Three structural challenges:

1 — Sectoral Silos Research is organized by sector — necessarily. But AI cascades operate across sectors. The hosting analyst tracks web hosting KPIs — the conversational AI developments that change user behavior sit in the tech analyst's domain. We need more cross-sector perspectives.

2 — Temporal Horizon Third-order effects with a 5–10 year time horizon are doubly discounted: in probability and present value. Methodologically sound — but AI timelines compress faster than historical technology cycles. The discount factors deserve a second look.

3 — Narrative Anchoring "AI replaces X jobs" is concrete and narratable — a good anchor for analysis. But cascade effects are more abstract and harder to embed in valuation models. The AI Threat Matrix provides a structured approach to capturing these effects systematically.

Small & Mid-Caps: Special Attention Required

Lower analyst coverage + less diversified business models + more constrained adaptation resources = the market segment where second- and third-order AI effects deserve the most analytical attention.

Bottom Line

The AI Threat Matrix is not a forecasting model. It is a diagnostic tool — an extension of our analytical framework. It supplements the first-order question we already answer well with two more:

  1. Which tasks can AI replace?
    (First order — the gas station. Analysis here is already well advanced.)
  2. How does AI change the behavior of customers, suppliers, and partners?
    (Second order — Suburbia. Here we need more cross-sector perspective.)
  3. Could AI reconfigure the entire market such that the business model is no longer relevant?
    (Third order — Los Angeles. Here we need to rethink.)
